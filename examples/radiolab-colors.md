# Radiolab 'Colors' Discussion Agenda  
  
Source Episode: [https://open.spotify.com/episode/7ugEsqx79eit9kFVw5xN7a](https://open.spotify.com/episode/7ugEsqx79eit9kFVw5xN7a) - Radiolab “Colors”.  
  
    
## A Subtle Fun Thing:  
  
In the episode Mic describes the sound of his feeling as like elevator music. As a subtle nod, play elevator music as guests arrive.   
  
  
  
## Arrival Activity - (Low Stakes Welcome Task)  
  
Name tag and pick a color swatch that you either absolutely love or absolutely hate. Then grab some food, and optionally ask others about their color swatch choice.  
  
Optional - [https://www.colorblindnesstest.org/farnsworth-munsell-100-hue-test/](https://www.colorblindnesstest.org/farnsworth-munsell-100-hue-test/)   
  
  
## Opening   
  
Let’s jump in. This episode covers a lot of ground—Newton, mantis shrimp, Homer, tetrachromats. Before we get into specifics, what stuck with you? What’s the thing you’re still thinking about?  
  
  
## Small Groups - Quick round  
  
Find someone you don’t know and we are going to pair off quickly:  
  
1. The episode described the different rainbows different animals see (dogs having two cones, mantis shrimp having 16, and some women have 4.) If you could pick one of those realities which one would you pick for yourself? Which one would you pick for your worst enemy?  
2. The episode argues we might **not** be able to see colors we don’t have words for. When have you had the experience of suddenly ‘seeing’ something that was always there—once someone gave you the language or framework for it?   
3. This episode showcased the debate of color being something in the physical world vs being something entirely made up by the mind. Which side of the debate do you think is more correct?   
  
  
## Full Group - Themes and Questions  
  
Get the whole group seated.   
  
“Anyone hear something interesting from their partner they want to share?”   
  
  
### The wine-dark sea:   
  
Homer called the sea "wine-dark”. He wasn't describing a color — he was describing a quality, maybe depth or luminosity.   
  
Is our obsession with precise color names actually a step forward, or did we lose something when we stopped describing the world in terms of feeling and texture?  
  
  
### The limits of more:   
  
The mantis shrimp has 16 color receptors but uses them as crude triggers—fight, eat, mate. In that case, more data didn’t equal more understanding.   
  
We have more information than any generation in history. Are we actually seeing more clearly?  
  
  
### The construction of reality:   
  
The Himba tribe can distinguish dozens of greens that look identical to us, but struggle to pick out a blue square among green ones.   
  
If perception is this culturally constructed, how much of our lives may be a false truth?  
  
### Isms - Sexism, Racism, Ageism, etc:   
  
Gladstone's first instinct was that Homer and all ancient Greeks were literally colorblind. He couldn't conceive that the gap was cultural, not biological.   
  
Where do we make that same mistake now — assuming someone who sees things differently has a deficiency rather than a different framework?  
  
### The violence of seeing:   
  
Tom Cronin described the mantis shrimp as being "into violence, not beauty" — 16 color receptors deployed entirely in service of killing, eating, and mating.   
  
Would beauty disappear if we could see everything?  
  
### The dormant cone:   
  
Seven of the eight women with the fourth cone couldn't use it. Only one — possibly because she worked with color professionally — had trained her brain to interpret the extra signal.   
  
What activates perception? Is it practice, attention, necessity, or something else?  
  
  
### The Victoria Finlay question:   
  
Finlay described how Newton's prism experiment "sucked the poetry out of the heavens" — once you know a rainbow is just refracted light, something is lost.   
  
When you learn the science behind something beautiful, does it gain or lose something for you?
